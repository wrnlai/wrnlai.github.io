Louvre Website Redesign

Date

December '18

Context

IBM UX Design Challenge

Background

The Louvre, or the Louvre Museum, is the world's largest art museum and a historic monument in Paris, France.

Challenge

IBM challenged me to redesign parts of a website for the world’s largest museum: The Louvre. The context is as shown: Your co-worker, a user researcher, has analytics that show many users are looking for information on prominent pieces of art, but are leaving before they find what they want. Propose changes to address this feedback.

Skills

  • User Experience Design

  • Prototyping

  • Interface Design

1. Research and Assumptions

Assumptions

Without input from the Louvre and their priorities, I set some general assumptions about the envisioned user and their website.

  1. People want to use this search feature for art search, I am assuming that all their logistical search queries (e.g ticket sales, planning your visit, and Louvre activities) have been satisfied.
  2. Assuming that the Louvre wants users to search their website for information, and that search is, from a business side, advantageous for ticket sales.
  3. The Louvre wants to create an experience that is both delightful and engaging for art enthusiasts.

The Louvre's encumbered search process. The total real estate alloted to search was less than 10% of the homepage. In addition, users are greeted with 4 different search pages to search on, further complicating the search experience.

Initial Observations

Having never been to the Louvre, or its website, I decided to begin by taking a few notes on the layout of the site, particularly focussing on how the information structure, emphasized elements, and the user flow through searching for an art piece. Being cognizant of my time, I gave myself about 30 minutes to sift through the site.

It seems the goal was to put everything about the website on the front page. You could find information on donating money, while simultaneously learning about which art pieces appeared in Beyonce and Jay Z’s music video. Right away, I had already felt cognitively overloaded from the information provided from the site.

In addition, there were an excessive use of tabs– some functioning as buttons, others as hover interactions.

Guerilla User Interview

With the time alloted, I decided to interview an art enthusiast to understand the process and motivations behind researching and understanding art.

2.
Initial Sketches and Concepts

Initial Sketches primarily focussed on capturing an immersive experience which emphasized visual components of art rather than text.

I intentionally kept the key elements in the sketches visually sparse in order to prevent any sort of cognitive overload within the experience.

3.
Interface Design

Search Page


To address the confusions about which pages to use for search, I designed a dedicated search page that could describe the limits and materials available for search on each page. The color themes are kept consistent throughout the search process.

Search Results


Prioritizing visuals over text, I sized up the visuals and presented them in a carousel. Users can scroll through large visuals to discover and learn about new art pieces.

Expanded Art Information


Included previous and next works to more realistically portray the art exploration process. Text is prevalent but does not distract the reader from the overall focus of the art.

4. Reflection

In retrospect


Given more time, I would have liked to user test this wireframe to understand both the strengths and shortcomings of this process. In addition, I would have also liked to incoroporate more of the Louvre’s business focuses into the design while crafting this delightful search experience.

Overall, the experience was actually quite fun, as I’ve never really looked into the experience of browsing a museum website.
