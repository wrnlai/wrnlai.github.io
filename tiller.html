Tiller

Date

September '19 - December '19

Position

Design Consultant, Sprint Leader for High Fidelity / User Testing Sprint.

Background

Tiller is a social network entirely dedicated to politics. Seeking to steer away from the political drama and fake news bred from traditional social networks, Tiller provides a safe, dedicated space for verified registered voters to have discussions and take actions.

Challenge

Through Berkeley Innovation, My team teamed up with Tiller to design a virtual ballot with a focus on educating voters on their candidates and propositions.

How might we build a digital, interactive and verified sample ballot that can dramatically help voters increase both their knowledge and power?

Skills

  • User Research/Usability Testing
  • User Experience Design
  • Prototyping

1.
User Research

Facts, Assumptions, and Questions

Though the challenge given seemed relatively straightforward, we soon realized that this project could become very open-ended. Each of my teammates had their own perspective on their voter experience; what needed to be fixed and enhanced was different between all of us, based on our own voting experiences. We decided to first conduct a “Facts, Assumptions, and Questions” ( FAQ) to explore each perspective and find any areas of overlap. After the FAQ, we also realized that we had more questions than answers at the moment, particularly concerning the reasons why politics and traditional social networks could not mesh. We then decided to conduct some secondary research on this matter, highlighting the populations response to social network politics.


User Interviews

We interviewed various people to understand how they learn about politics and how comfortable they are with expressing their opinions ***TO ADD****

What we learned

  1. Voting behavior among all voting groups showed that it was nonlinear. Voters would most likely review previously researched candidates to compare and contrast.

  2. An uninformed vote often leads to a devaluation of the voting process. Voters who have not educated themselves on their candidates felt that their vote didn’t matter.

  3. Voters desired a central source of objective political information, as opposed to having the back-and-forth between different news sources.

  4. Unsurprisingly, voters were very timid about voicing their political stances, only because they did not want to misjudged or be viewed within the frame of their vote. Yet, voters had no problem voicing the fact that they were politically active (‘I Voted!’)

6.
High Fidelity

Homepage

The homepage of the repository welcomes each user with up-to-date research. Drawing inspiration from online journals, the homepage communicates an institution rich with research activity to the users.

Search Overlay

The search overlay is accessible from the header of any page, allowing users to look up topics and find relevant research materials quickly and easily.

Search Results

Displays research materials and author profiles from the institution. Allows users to filter results for specific search queries.

Works Page

Displays key pieces of information describing the research material. Researchers can use this page to cite, download, or overview the work.

Author Page

Authors within the institution can create a dedicated profile page where they can be discovered by the larger scientific community. Authors can customize this to showcase their academic interests and other works they’ve contributed to.
