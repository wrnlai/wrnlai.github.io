1. Foreword

What is a repository?

In academia, it is common practice for the Institution to showcase their research to the general public to promote the quality work of their researchers. This allows academics to publish their content without having to deal with the hassle of publishing through a major publication. In addition, it serves to build the institution’s research influence in the broader academic community.

Anything from masters theses, archived art, datasets, to journal publications can be found in a centralized database we call a repository.

What was our role?

Ubiquity Press recently partnered with several research institutions in American and London to host their open access repositories.

Together with Mattin Delavar, we were given the task to create a repository framework that would be highly customizable for any research institution to use. Although there was a MVP prototype in testing, we were tasked to approach with fresh eyes to create a new design that emphasized usability and reduced friction.

2.
Initial Research and Needfinding

Competitive Analysis

Given our limited knowledge of repository products and academia, we dove into research for the first few days of the project. We analyzed over 9 repositories from various institutions and were particularly interested in:

  1. The information architecture structured within repositories – what pieces of information were most prominently displayed? Is there a clear hierarchy?

  2. The current usability of the site — how did each r epository accomplish core functions like searching and browsing?

  3. Pain points — were there any areas of friction, or information overload?

  4. Common practices among academia — what patterns were found across multiple repositories? Were users already adapted to a certain look and feel of a product?


User Interviews

During our internship, we were also given the opportunity to conduct 6 user interviews with academics and repository managers from various institutions. Here we dove deeper to explore different user tasks and user goals from both groups. It was especially useful for us to learn about what they percieved the repository to be used for.

3.
User Flow

How we designed our user flow

From our research, we found that most people conceptualized the r epository into two functionalities: Search and Browse. While Search was for those with either very specific queries, it was meant for users who wanted to broaden their search from a single point.
Vice versa, the browse functionality were for users to get a glance from a broad level, and then converge onto a single category of search (i.e “Masters Theses from the Computer Science Department”). We decided to change the name of this functionlaity to Explore, as we felt that it captured the experience much more accurately.
We began designing our userflow from the homepage, as most pages revolved around this and then split the main functionality into its two core functionalities

6.
High Fidelity

Homepage

The homepage of the repository welcomes each user with up-to-date research. Drawing inspiration from online journals, the homepage communicates an institution rich with research activity to the users.

Search Overlay

The search overlay is accessible from the header of any page, allowing users to look up topics and find relevant research materials quickly and easily.

Search Results

Displays research materials and author profiles from the institution. Allows users to filter results for specific search queries.

Works Page

Displays key pieces of information describing the research material. Researchers can use this page to cite, download, or overview the work.

Author Page

Authors within the institution can create a dedicated profile page where they can be discovered by the larger scientific community. Authors can customize this to showcase their academic interests and other works they’ve contributed to.
