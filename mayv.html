Brief

Working from scratch, my team and I created a novel mobile experience that could assist chronic pain patients navigating through alternative medicine. We worked under tight deadlines – from defining our goals, carving out product strategy, to executing interface designs for their mobile experience.

As a design lead, I was knee-deep in the entire process– from driving product design , facilitating collaboration between the founders, to executing design on the home page, tracking tool, and onboarding flow.

Problem

Navigating the world of alternative medicine is daunting.

Patients experiencing chronic pain are severely limited by joint pain, and are mired with a surrounding health issues (e.g declining mental health). Patients are looking at alternative medicines and therapies to treat their pain; however, navigating towards alternative medicine is unclear.

  • Long journey to diagnosis– takes years, lots of misdiagnoses, and blends with other autoimmune symptoms.

  • Disconnected Resources– lots of legwork for the patient to find alternative treatments.

  • Adjusting health habits difficult– for anyone, it’s hard to change your routine, and even harder to maintain complex treatment plans.

    Onboarding

    To help patients get started with the app, they are quickly introduced to the primary benefits and mission statement of the app. The patient also fills out a survey to curate tailor the resources to their symptoms and conditions.

    Home

    Quickly access all of the resources Mayv offers. Displays the patient’s most frequented meditations, relevant articles to their condition, and other tools.

    My Program

    Houses the core education materials within the app. Patients can quickly access lessons from their 13-week program or tap into meditation exercises. Each lesson is outlined to set teaching expectations and a step-by-step walkthrough.

    Discover

    The app carefully curates information that is well-documented and credible for patients to learn more. Patients can read through surveys, patient stories, and other articles.

    Symptom Tracking

    Patients can monitor the behavior and progress of their condition over time. This can be used to help pinpoint triggers to flareups and compare the intensity of different symptoms during their progression.

    Symptom Logging

    Patients can quickly log specific symptoms at any point in their day. The logs use a combination of likert scales and multiple choice questions to help patients elaborate their condition. Patients can also quickly view their past responses to compare and contrast logs. At the end, if possible, the app will surface relevant articles based on their symptoms.

    Research sprint

    Understanding life with chronic pain

    Our research sprint spanned 2 weeks, but trickled through the rest of the semester. To ensure time was used efficiently, we selected methods that could be feasibly executed while getting a diverse spread of information.

    Literature

    We delved into literature to understand how autoimmune patients cope with their condition and how alternative medicine techniques are assimilated into a patient’s lifestyle. There was also a handful of literature dedicated to the analysis of therapeutic apps.

    • Patients preferred easy tap user interfaces to multitap or slider methods, and vertical question layouts.

    • Patients liked to be engaged by progress feedback reports and educational tips.

    • Avoid dark design patterns (addiction, social anxiety, purchase patterns).

    • Designing for mindfulness: “Promote a calm experience by providing a sense of completion and giving users an option to disengage”.

    Competitive Analyses

    We also looked into the medical/healthcare apps and reviews to examine whether existing products could align with our focus.

    • Patients want to track their growth over time and receive motivation from the application.

    • Many apps had significant experiences dedicated to educating patients on their condition through mini lessons, articles, and surveys.

    • Patients want to receive catered feedback based on their inputs (symptoms, logs, surveys).

    User Interviews

    We conducted interviews with autoimmune patients from across the country. Through these conversations, we learned about their day-to-day habits, and their personal experiences coping with chronic pain and flare-ups.

    • Source of information came from multiple sources, including articles, medical journals, social media, and TV shows.

    • A lot of uncertainty and anxiety regarding CBD (access, legality, effects).

    • The disease can alter personality and/or cause depression.

    Ideating and converging

    Scoping out our problem

    After our research sprint, we were ready to apply our learnings to the project. However, we quickly realized that our individual conceptions of our goal were vague and not flushed out at the time. We also wanted to ensure that our design team and the client’s goals were unified before we could make any design decisions.

    To achieve this clarity, we used a framing exercise to focus on the specific problem our app is trying to address. With the team and client, we sat down and listed all of our conceptions of the solutions, constraints, and ultimate impact of this app. Afterwards, we converged on a unified “How Might We” question and a centered design goal to work toward.

    How might we create a supportive, empathetic, and realistic environment [for patients] that pairs education with community to guide chronic pain patients through the ups and downs of their condition?

    Ideation

    With all of our research findings, we invited our client to brainstorm features together. We would be taking everything we learned from our competitive analyses, interviews, and literature reviews.

    Together, we came up with as many ideas as possible without considering feasibility at this stage. As we progressed, we began grouping into wider commonaltiies such as “education, ad-hoc tools, community, etc.”

    Converging with a problem map

    To converge on these features, we shifted our ideation session around a problem map centered around the patient. To begin, we listed the most apparent problems that chronic pain patients faced based on our research, and used those as platforms to anchor our features. We also considered other factors such as feasibility, and the value/impact on patients.

    For example, although body pain map tracking seemed to be a great way for patients to track their condition, we felt the interactions and data produced might be too complex for patients to gain insight from.

    Core Functionality

    After converging on our features, we defined these core features to describe how the different aspects of the mobile app should be designed in order to support the patient.

    Centralized information

    Providing a space where patients can explore alternative medicine with credible information. Patients can opt-in to different topics at any time, choosing to learn as they go.

    Tools and prompts for self reflection

    Encouraging patients to convert their learnings into constructive habits they can apply in their lives. These lessons aren’t meant to be rushed through, but require introspection and assimilation.

    Self tracking

    Patients can quickly log down information about their symptoms, triggers, and habits over time to stay informed on their condition.

    Patients self-discover insights

    To complement self-tracking, patients can visualize their condition over time, and find trends and correlations. Visualizations are annotated to show appropriate events and can be superimposed with other condtions.

    Initial Prototyping

    Early sketches and prototyping focused on hierarchy and prioritizing user actions. Without any prior screens, we used this design sprint to nail down our highest priority elements and create basic wireframes that embodied our user flows. Given the unique structure of this program and demographic, we strove to create a interface that could easily navigate through the program and motivate patients to complete the program at an encouraged pace.

    Crafting a design system

    Our team worked to create a design system that could suit mobile experiences, while staying in line with our brand guidelines.

    Typography

    The brand solely relied on one typeface and one weight, Solanel Regular. The neo-grotesque font featured flawless curves evoking calm tones. While we were experimenting with the different sizes, it was difficult to produce enough contrast to distinguish between different headers and body fonts. We decided to introduce a bolder weight into the overall design, reserving use for headers and buttons.

    Gridding

    When designing the gridding, we wanted to create a system that had a balanced whitespace:content ratio. Going back to our mindfulness principles, it was important to restrain the amount of content being displayed in one screen.

    Design Iterations

    For the later half of the semester, we honed our wireframes towards high fidelity. Each week recieved a new set of iterations, based on client and team critique.

    Lesson of the Day

    The “Lesson of the Day” element is the main entry point for patients going into their lesson. It aims to set a healthy expectation of what a patient should finish in a day and provide a metric of progress. Initially, we experimented with different call-to-action styles and different progress indicators.

    As we progressed, we grouped the “Lesson” and “Reflection” portion of the program into one call-to-action button and showcased a variety of meditation cards. Language of the “Lesson” button was changed from “Watch” to “Begin” to adapt for both video and audio lessons.

    Education → My Program

    The “Education” section experienced the most change. Initially, it began as a page with two tabs, splitting “Education” from “Meditations”. Meditations were separate from the education, but unlocked as the patient’s progressed through the lessons. A constant critique was that it was not clear where to find the meditations from the navigation bar.

    Although we understood this complication, we also did not believe that “Meditations” was a large enough feature to have its own section on the nav bar. Instead, we decided to rename “Education” into “Your Program” to generalize the contents of this section. Furthermore, we got rid of the 2-tab system and split the two sections vertically. We were cognizant of reducing screen real estate, and making sure that both the Lessons and Meditations were visible above the fold of the screen.

    Tweaking the self tracking

    Our tracking method was cumbersome, with no way to specify symptoms that a patient wanted to record on that day. Rather, it presented all of the possible symptoms in one endlessly scrolling survey.

    To add more intention to this design, we broke up the self tracking into a multi-page approach, beginning with a prompt to select the symptoms they’d like to record. Another given benefit of this change was that it introduced more friction into the experience, giving patients more space to contemplate each symptom, rather than breezing through the form. Furthermore, we displayed short descriptions for each of the descriptors on the likert scales.

    Looking back at our progress

    This was my first time designing in the role of a design lead. In addition to building my skills in UI/UX design, this project challenged me to think deeply about fostering an environment for a design team and my role overseeing the design of the product. It wasn’t easy – there were many lessons learned. But in the end, we pushed through, and I’m thankful for this immense learning opportunity.

    Ensuring design consistency

    Consistency in design extends past the visual style guide. Does each interface transition seamlessly across different features? If components are being utilized, is the interaction also consistent? These questions are especially important to consider when designers are working separately on different features. At times, it may seem logical for one to design interfaces to best house a specific function. However, it’s important to consider how this design sits within the overall experience, and the friction that comes from introducing different contexts.

    Embracing Uncertainty

    Despite having run through the human-centered design proces s a couple of times now, I realize that the process is much messier, especially in a start-up environment. This uncertainty taught us the importance of placing intentionality in the methods that we used and to constantly reflect on the effectiveness of our methods.

    Client Communication

    As I began to learn the product inside and out, I found myself bringing forward meaningful conversations that challenged the clients to think about the experience differently. In many cases, the key point is being able to show how your design decisions translate into the bigger picture of the product.

    Team Communication

    I have learned that great designers are not only good at design itself; they’re also people who can influence others, who can help others on the team achieve their goals. Throughout this project, I practiced to provide criticism constructively and clearly. As this project comes to a close, communication is one of the skills that I will continually work on improving.

    As expected, there were a fair number of learning opportunities too

    Timing

    Our biggest challenge came down to the timing when scoping out this project. With only 15 weeks, I’d thought we’d be able to run an end-to-end design project, but at the end, we barely finished prototyping. User testing was pushed to be done in the summer after the semester ended.

    Tracing back our steps

    In our process, I overlooked some design decisions that came back and haunted us later on down the road. Some examples:

    • Typography and style guide was not clearly set before going into hi-fi.

    • Health tracking/ symptom logging’s user flow was not developed enough to be prototyped. We realized there were a plethora of complex use cases that had not been addressed, but were too constrained on time / too far in build it through.



    Some of the issues we encountered can be traced back to our short window of 15 weeks to complete the project. If I could time travel and redo this project with the knowledge I have today, I would build in more milestones during the early prototyping stage that would allow the team to assess progress and solidify our design direction.
