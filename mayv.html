Problem

Navigating through alternative medicine is daunting.

Patients experiencing chronic pain are severely limited by joint pain, and is often coupled with other health issues such as declining mental health. Patients are looking at alternative medicines and therapies to treat their pain. However, navigating towards alternative medicine is unclear.

i. Long journey to diagnosis

Takes years, lots of misdiagnosis, blends with other autoimmune symptoms.

ii. Disconnected Resources

Lots of legwork for the patient to find alternative treatments.

iii. Adjusting health habits difficult

For anyone it‚Äôs hard to change your routine, and even harder to main complex treatment plans.

Mayv is a a resilience-based chronic pain management program. It seeks to bridge a connection for patients to access alternative medicine. The 13-week program combines mindfulness training with cognitive behavioral therapy frameworks.

As their design team, We designed their mobile experience to deliver this program to patients.

research sprint

Understanding what it‚Äôs like to live with chronic pain.

Our research sprint spanned 2 weeks, but trickled through. We wanted to use this time well and selected methods that were could be feasibly executed while getting a diverse spread of information.

Based on our research we derived three design goals that Ubiquity Repositories is based on.

Literature

We delved into literature to understand how autoimmune patients cope with their condition, and how integrative medicine techniques are assimilated into a patient‚Äôs lifestyle. There was also an handful of literature dedicated to the analysis of therapeutic apps.

  • patients preferred easy tap user interfaces to multitap or slider methods, and vertical question layouts

  • patients liked to be engaged by progress feedback reports and educational tips

  • avoiding dark design patterns (addiction, social anxiety, purchase patterns)

  • patients weigh clinical treatments (drugs, surgical procedures) and alternative medicine (physical therapy, massages, acupuncture, CBT, CBD) equally

Competitive Analyses

We also looked into the medical/healthcare apps to examine whether existing products could align with our focus.

  • users want to track their growth over time and receive motivation from the application

  • applications that create and form communities promote continued growth and loyalty

  • users seek connections and bonds with other members, even through anonymity

  • users want to receive specialized, catered feedback based on their inputs (symptoms, logs, surveys)

  • users want to evolve with their disease and partake in new educational content

User Interviews

We conducted interviews with autoimmune patients from across the country. Through these conversations, we learned about their day-to-day habits, and their personal experiences coping with chronic pain and flareups.

  • source of information came from multiple sources, including articles, medical journals, social media, and TV shows

  • patients without strong support groups from friends or family reached out to online communities and forums

  • patients were fearful of pain and felt the need to control environments in case of flare-ups

  • lots of uncertainty and anxiety regarding CBD (access, legality, effects)

  • patients are skeptical of mindfulness techniques, but are willing to give them a try

  • - the disease changes your personality

Ideating and Converging

Scoping out our problem

After our research sprint, we were ready to apply our learnings to the project. However, we quickly realized that our individual conceptions of our goal were vague and not flushed out at the time. We also wanted to ensure that our design team and the client‚Äôs goals were tied together before we could make any design decisions.

To achieve this clarity, we used a framing exercise to focus on the specific problem our app is trying to address. With the team and client, we sat down and listed all of our conceptions of the solutions, constraints, and ultimate impact of this app. Afterwards, we converged on a unified ‚ÄúHow Might We‚Äù question and a centered design goal to work toward.

HMW create a supportive, empathetic, realistic but optimistic environment [for patients] that pairs education with community to guide chronic pain patients through the ups and downs of their condition?

Initial Prototyping

Early sketches and prototyping focused on hierarchy and prioritizing user actions. Without any prior screens, we used this design sprint to nail down our highest priority elements and create basic wireframes that embodied our user flows. Given the unique structure of this program and demographic, we strove to create a interface that could easily navigate through the program, and motivate patients to complete the program at the encouraged pace.

Design Iterations

For the later half of the semester, we honed our wireframes towards high fidelity. Each week recieved a new set of iterations, based on client and team critique.

Lesson of the Day

The lesson of the day element is the key entry points for patients going into their lesson. It aimed to set a healthy expectation of what a patient should finish today and provide a metric of progress for motivation. Initially, we experimented with different call-to-action styles, and different progress indicators.

As we progressed, we grouped the Lesson and Reflection portion of the program into one call-to-action button, and provided a variety of meditation cards, rather than an ambiguous call-to-action button. Language of the lesson button was changed from ‚ÄúWatch‚Äù to ‚ÄúBegin‚Äù to adapt to both video and audio lessons.

Education ‚Üí My Program

The education section experienced the most change. Initially, it began as a page with two tabs, splitting education from meditations. Meditations were separate from the education, but unlocked as the patient‚Äôs progressed through the lessons. A constant critique was that it was not clear where to find the meditations from the navigation bar.

Although we understood this complication, we also did not believe that meditation was a large enough feature to have its own section on the navigation. Instead, we decided to rename ‚ÄúEducation‚Äù into ‚ÄúYour Program‚Äù to generalize the contents of this section. Furthermore, we got rid of the 2-tab system and split the two sections vertically. We were cognizant of reducing screen real estate, and making sure that both the Lessons and Meditations were visible above the fold of the screen.

Tweaking the self tracking.

One common problem we found with our tracking method was that it was long and cumbersome, with no way to select the specific symptoms that a patient wanted to record on that day. Rather, it would present all of the possible symptoms in one endlessly scrolling survey.

To give more intention to the patient, we broke up the self tracking into a ulti-page approach, beginning with a prompt to select the symptoms they‚Äôd like to record. Another given benefit of this change was that it introduced more friction into the experience, giving patients more space to contemplate each symptom, rather than breezing through the form.

Onboarding

To help patients get started with the app, they are quickly introduced to the primary benefits and mission statement of the app. The patient also fills out a survey to curate tailor the resources to their symptoms and conditions.

Home

Quickly access all of the resources Mayv offers. Displays the patient‚Äôs most frequented meditations, relevant articles to their, and other tools.

My Program

Houses the core eudcation materials within the app. Patients can quickly access lessons from their 13-week program or tap into meditation exercises. Each lesson is outlined to set teaching expectations and a step-by-step walkthrough.

Discover

The app carefully curates information that is well-documented and credible for patients to learn more. Patients can read through surveys, patient stories, and other articles.

Symptom Tracking

Patients can monitor the behavior and progress of their condition over time. This can be used to help pinpoint triggers to flareups, and compare the intensity of different symptoms during their progression.

Symptom Logging

Patients can quickly log specific symptoms at any point in their day. The logs use a combination of likert scales and multiple choice questions to help patients elaborate their condition. Patients can also quickly view their past responses to compare and contrast logs. At the end, if possible, the app will surface relevant articles based on their symptoms.
