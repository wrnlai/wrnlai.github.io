Problem

Navigating through alternative medicine is daunting.

Patients experiencing chronic pain are severely limited by joint pain, and is often coupled with other health issues such as declining mental health. Patients are looking at alternative medicines and therapies to treat their pain. However, navigating towards alternative medicine is unclear.

i. Long journey to diagnosis

Takes years, lots of misdiagnosis, blends with other autoimmune symptoms.

ii. Disconnected Resources

Lots of legwork for the patient to find alternative treatments.

iii. Adjusting health habits difficult

For anyone it’s hard to change your routine, and even harder to main complex treatment plans.

Mayv is a a resilience-based chronic pain management program. It seeks to bridge a connection for patients to access alternative medicine. The 13-week program combines mindfulness training with cognitive behavioral therapy frameworks.

As their design team, we designed their mobile experience to deliver this program to patients.

Onboarding

To help patients get started with the app, they are quickly introduced to the primary benefits and mission statement of the app. The patient also fills out a survey to curate tailor the resources to their symptoms and conditions.

Home

Quickly access all of the resources Mayv offers. Displays the patient’s most frequented meditations, relevant articles to their condition, and other tools.

My Program

Houses the core education materials within the app. Patients can quickly access lessons from their 13-week program or tap into meditation exercises. Each lesson is outlined to set teaching expectations and a step-by-step walkthrough.

Discover

The app carefully curates information that is well-documented and credible for patients to learn more. Patients can read through surveys, patient stories, and other articles.

Symptom Tracking

Patients can monitor the behavior and progress of their condition over time. This can be used to help pinpoint triggers to flareups and compare the intensity of different symptoms during their progression.

Symptom Logging

Patients can quickly log specific symptoms at any point in their day. The logs use a combination of likert scales and multiple choice questions to help patients elaborate their condition. Patients can also quickly view their past responses to compare and contrast logs. At the end, if possible, the app will surface relevant articles based on their symptoms.

research sprint

Understanding what it’s like to live with chronic pain.

Our research sprint spanned 2 weeks, but trickled through the rest of the semester. We wanted to use this time well and selected methods that could be feasibly executed while getting a diverse spread of information.

Literature

We delved into literature to understand how autoimmune patients cope with their condition, and how integrative medicine techniques are assimilated into a patient’s lifestyle. There was also a handful of literature dedicated to the analysis of therapeutic apps.

  • patients preferred easy tap user interfaces to multitap or slider methods, and vertical question layouts

  • patients liked to be engaged by progress feedback reports and educational tips

  • avoiding dark design patterns (addiction, social anxiety, purchase patterns)

  • Desinging for mindfulness: “Promote a calm experience by providing a sense of completion and giving users an option to disengage.”

Competitive Analyses

We also looked into the medical/healthcare apps and reviews to examine whether existing products could align with our focus.

  • Users want to track their growth over time and receive motivation from the application

  • Virtual pain coaches were widely popular, but had limitations (+ a little creepy).

  • Many apps had significant experiences dedicated to educating patients on their condition through mini lessons, articles, and surveys.

  • users seek connections and bonds with other members, even through anonymity

  • users want to receive specialized, catered feedback based on their inputs (symptoms, logs, surveys)

  • users want to evolve with their disease and partake in new educational content

User Interviews

We conducted interviews with autoimmune patients from across the country. Through these conversations, we learned about their day-to-day habits, and their personal experiences coping with chronic pain and flareups.

  • source of information came from multiple sources, including articles, medical journals, social media, and TV shows

  • patients without strong support groups from friends or family reached out to online communities and forums

  • patients were fearful of pain and felt the need to control environments in case of flare-ups

  • lots of uncertainty and anxiety regarding CBD (access, legality, effects)

  • patients are skeptical of mindfulness techniques, but are willing to give them a try

Ideating and Converging

Scoping out our problem

After our research sprint, we were ready to apply our learnings to the project. However, we quickly realized that our individual conceptions of our goal were vague and not flushed out at the time. We also wanted to ensure that our design team and the client’s goals were tied together before we could make any design decisions.

To achieve this clarity, we used a framing exercise to focus on the specific problem our app is trying to address. With the team and client, we sat down and listed all of our conceptions of the solutions, constraints, and ultimate impact of this app. Afterwards, we converged on a unified “How Might We” question and a centered design goal to work toward.

How might we create a supportive, empathetic, realistic but optimistic environment [for patients] that pairs education with community to guide chronic pain patients through the ups and downs of their condition?

Ideation

With all of our research findings, I invited our designers and our client to brainstorm features together. We would be taking everything we learned from our competitive analyses, interviews, and literature reviews.

Together, we came up with as many ideas as possible without considering feasibility at this stage. As we progressed, we began grouping into wider commonaltiies such as “education, ad-hoc tools, community, etc.”

Converging with a problem map

To converge on these features, we shifted our ideation session around a problem map centered around the patient. To begin, we listed the most apparent problems that chronic pain patients faced based on our research, and used those as platforms to anchor our features. We also considered other factors such as feasibility, and the value/impact on patients.

For example, although body pain map tracking seemed to be a great way for patients to track their condition, we felt the interactions and data produced might be too complex for patients to gain insight from.

Core Functionality

We defined these core features to describe how the different aspects of the mobile app should be designed in order to support the patient.

📚 Centralized Information

Providing a space where patients can explore alternative medicine with credible information. Patients can opt-in to different topics at any time, choosing to learn as they go.

🧘🏻‍♀️ Tools and Prompts for Self Reflection:

Encouraging patients to convert their learnings into constructive habits they can apply in their lives. These lessons aren’t meant to be rushed through, but require introspection and assimilation.

✏️ Self Tracking

Patients can quickly log down information about their symptoms, triggers, and habits over time to stay informed on their condition.

🧠 Patients Self-discover Insights

To complement self-tracking, patients can visualize their condition over time, and find trends and correlations. Visualizations are annotated to show appropriate events and can be superimposed with other condtions.

Initial Prototyping

Early sketches and prototyping focused on hierarchy and prioritizing user actions. Without any prior screens, we used this design sprint to nail down our highest priority elements and create basic wireframes that embodied our user flows. Given the unique structure of this program and demographic, we strove to create a interface that could easily navigate through the program, and motivate patients to complete the program at the encouraged pace.

Crafting a design system

Our team worked to create a design system that could suit mobile experiences, while staying in line with the marketing design guidelines.

Typography

The brand solely relied on one typeface and one weight, Solanel Regular. The font was neo-grotesque with flawless curves, evoking calm tones. While we were experimenting with the different sizes, it was difficult to produce enough contrast to distinguish between different headers and body fonts. We decided to introduce a second weight into the overall design, reserving use for headers and buttons.

Gridding

When designing the gridding, we wanted to create a system that had a balanced whitespace:content ratio. Going back to our mindfulness principles, it was important to restrain the amount of content being displayed in one screen.

Design Iterations

For the later half of the semester, we honed our wireframes towards high fidelity. Each week recieved a new set of iterations, based on client and team critique.

Lesson of the Day

The lesson of the day element is the key entry point for patients going into their lesson. It aims to set a healthy expectation of what a patient should finish today and provide a metric of progress for motivation. Initially, we experimented with different call-to-action styles, and different progress indicators.

As we progressed, we grouped the Lesson and Reflection portion of the program into one call-to-action button, and provided a variety of meditation cards, rather than an ambiguous call-to-action button. Language of the lesson button was changed from “Watch” to “Begin” to adapt to both video and audio lessons.

Education → My Program

Initially, the education began as a page with two tabs, splitting education from meditations. A constant critique was that it was not clear where to find the meditations from the navigation bar. The use of tabs was also widely inconsistent among the other features in the app.

Although we accepted this critique, we also did not believe that meditations was a large-enough feature to have its own section on the navigation. Instead, we decided to rename “Education” into “Your Program” to generalize the contents of this section. Furthermore, we got rid of the 2-tab system and split the two sections vertically. We were cognizant of reducing screen real estate, and making sure that both the Lessons and Meditations were visible above the fold of the screen.

Tweaking the self tracking.

One common problem we found with our tracking method was that it was long and cumbersome, with no way to select the specific symptoms that a patient wanted to record on that day. Rather, it would present all of the possible symptoms in one endlessly scrolling survey.

To give more intention to the patient, we broke up the self tracking into a ulti-page approach, beginning with a prompt to select the symptoms they’d like to record. Another given benefit of this change was that it introduced more friction into the experience, giving patients more space to contemplate each symptom, rather than breezing through the form.

Looking back at our progress.

This was my first time designing a new product as a design lead. In addition to building my skills in UI/UX design, it also challenged me to think deeply about team dynamics and the importance of team communication and critique. It wasn’t easy – there were lots of failures and lessons learned. But in the end, we pushed through, and I’m thankful for this immense learning opportunity.

Embracing Uncertainty

Despite having run through the human-centered design process a couple of times now, I realize that the process is much messier, especially regarding design in a start-up environment. This uncertainty taught us the importance of placing intentionality in the methods that we used, and to constantly reflect on the effectiveness of our processes.

Client Communication

As I began to learn the product inside and out, I found myself bringing forward meaningful conversations that challenged the clients to think about the experience differently. In many cases, the key point is being able to show how your design decisions translate into the bigger picture of the product.

Team Communication

I have learned that great designers are not only good at design itself; they’re also people who can influence others, who can help others on the team achieve their goals. Throughout this project, I practiced to provide criticism constructively and clearly. As this project comes to a close, communication is one of the skills that I will continually work on improving.

As expected, there were a fair number of failures too...

Timing

Much of it came down to timing when scoping out this project. With only 15 weeks, I’d thought we’d be able to run an end to end design project. We barely finished prototyping. User testing was pushed to be done in the Summer after the semester ended.

Tracing back our steps

In our process, I overlooked some design decisions, which came back to bite us in the ass. (e.g typography and style guide was not clearly set before going into hi-fi) (e.g health tracking/ symptom logging’s user flow was not developed enough to be prototyped. We realized there were a plethora of complex use cases that had not been addressed, but were too constrained on time / too far in build it through.)

Some of of these failures can be traced to time constraints, no doubt. However, if I had to do it again, I’d allocate some meetings to reflecting on our progress so far, and analyzing areas that were glossed over and adapting our process together.
