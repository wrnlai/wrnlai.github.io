1. Foreword

What is a repository?

In academia, it is common practice for the Institution to showcase their research to the general public to promote the quality work of their researchers. This allows academics to publish their content without having to deal with the hassle of publishing through a major publication. In addition, it serves to build the institution’s research influence in the broader academic community.

Anything from masters theses, archived art, datasets, to journal publications can be found in a centralized database we call a repository.

What was our role?

Ubiquity Press recently partnered with several research institutions in American and London to host their open access repositories.

Together with Mattin Delavar, we were given the task to create a repository framework that would be highly customizable for any research institution to use. Although there was a MVP prototype in testing, we were tasked to approach with fresh eyes to create a new design that emphasized usability and reduced friction.

2.
Initial Research and Needfinding

Competitive Analysis

Given our limited knowledge of repository products and academia, we dove into research for the first few days of the project. We analyzed over 9 repositories from various institutions and were particularly interested in:

  1. The information architecture structured within repositories – what pieces of information were most prominently displayed? Is there a clear hierarchy?

  2. The current usability of the site — how did each r epository accomplish core functions like searching and browsing?

  3. Pain points — were there any areas of friction, or information overload?

  4. Common practices among academia — what patterns were found across multiple repositories? Were users already adapted to a certain look and feel of a product?
